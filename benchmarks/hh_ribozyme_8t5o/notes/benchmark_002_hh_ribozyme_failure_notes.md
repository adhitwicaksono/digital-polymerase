# Benchmark 002: HH Ribozyme RNA → HNA Failure Notes

**Project:** Digital Polymerase  
**Broader context:** XNA World Project  
**Benchmark:** 002  
**Input case:** HH-type I ribozyme RNA structure  
**Conversion attempted:** RNA → HNA  
**Status:** Productive failure / archived stress test  
**Core finding:** Short-mer RNA → HNA logic does not generalize directly to larger folded RNA structures.

---

## Executive Summary

Benchmark 002 documents an early attempt to apply RNA → HNA conversion logic to a larger folded RNA structure: an HH-type I ribozyme.

The benchmark followed the initial success of **Prototype 001**, where a short RNA 8-mer was converted into an HNA-like candidate using an experimental HNA 8-mer template. That success was based on template-guided reconstruction: the HNA backbone was taken from the template, while RNA bases were aligned and transplanted onto the HNA backbone.

When this idea was applied to a larger folded ribozyme, the method failed to produce a validated HNA model.

A later structure-preserving workaround attempted to maintain the global RNA fold by modifying sugar/backbone atoms locally, but this should be interpreted only as a **pseudo-HNA local perturbation model**, not a true HNA ribozyme conversion.

The benchmark is useful because it defines an important boundary for Digital Polymerase:

> Short oligomer reconstruction and folded RNA conversion are different algorithmic problems.

## Background

Prototype 001 demonstrated a successful short-mer proof of concept:

```text
RNA 8-mer
+
HNA 8-mer template
+
template-guided reconstruction
=
candidate HNA-like 8-mer
```

The successful method avoided naive atom insertion and instead used:

- experimental HNA backbone geometry
- RNA base preservation
- local anchor alignment
- Kabsch transformation
- base transplantation onto the HNA backbone

Benchmark 002 tested whether this approach could scale to a larger RNA fold.

---

## Main Question

Can a larger folded RNA structure be converted into an HNA-like candidate while preserving its global fold and local chemical plausibility?

More specifically:

- Can a short-template approach scale to a folded ribozyme?
- Can local sugar/backbone edits preserve base stacking and tertiary structure?
- Can a large pseudo-HNA output be interpreted as a meaningful HNA candidate?
- What failure modes appear when moving from short oligomers to folded RNAs?

---

## Attempt 1: Scaling the Short-Template Logic

### Method

The first strategy attempted to apply the earlier template-guided RNA → HNA logic to the larger HH ribozyme input.

Conceptually:

```text
HNA template geometry
+
RNA ribozyme base information
+
local coordinate transformation
=
large HNA-like candidate
```

### Why It Failed

The short 8-mer method depends on having a comparable target HNA template.

For the HH ribozyme, this condition was not met.

Problems:

- no full-length HNA ribozyme template was available
- short HNA geometry could not represent the full folded RNA topology
- local residue conversion did not guarantee global backbone coherence
- large RNA tertiary structure imposed constraints that the short-mer method did not address
- base stacking, backbone direction, and chain continuity became harder to preserve simultaneously

### Lesson

Template-guided reconstruction requires compatible template coverage.

A short target template may be useful for local geometry, but it cannot automatically define a complete large folded XNA structure.

---

## Attempt 2: Structure-Preserving Local Sugar Modification

### Method

A second strategy attempted to preserve the original RNA fold by editing each residue locally.

The intended strategy was:

```text
keep C1′ fixed
keep base positions fixed
mostly preserve C3′, C4′, C5′ and backbone trajectory
insert or adjust HNA-like sugar/backbone atoms
modify O4′ and C2′ locally
export pseudo-HNA-like structure
```

### What Worked

This approach was useful because it tried to preserve:

- global RNA fold
- base positions
- base stacking
- C1′ base-attachment points
- approximate backbone trajectory

It may be useful as a local perturbation test.

### Why It Is Not a Successful HNA Conversion

Despite preserving the apparent fold, this method is not a validated HNA reconstruction.

Major issues:

1. **It is an in-place local edit.**  
   It modifies RNA coordinates locally rather than rebuilding from a connected HNA backbone template.

2. **RNA-like geometry may remain.**  
   Local editing can leave source-polymer features embedded in the output.

3. **Residue labels can overclaim chemistry.**  
   Changing labels does not guarantee that correct chemical atoms, stereochemistry, or topology were produced.

4. **No energy minimization was performed.**  
   Bond lengths, angles, torsions, and clashes were not physically relaxed.

5. **No force-field validation was performed.**  
   The output is not MD-ready.

6. **Connectivity may be ambiguous.**  
   Nonstandard residues may require explicit `CONECT` records or topology files.

7. **Function is not implied.**  
   The output gives no evidence of HNA ribozyme folding, catalysis, stability, or replication.

### Better Interpretation

The output should be described as:

```text
fold-preserving pseudo-HNA local perturbation model
```

not:

```text
converted HNA ribozyme
```

## Conversion-Level Classification

Digital Polymerase recognizes four conversion levels:

1. **Symbolic conversion**  
   Sequence or residue-label rewriting.

2. **Topological conversion**  
   Reassignment of residue identity, atom names, backbone topology, and linkage patterns.

3. **Geometric reconstruction**  
   Candidate 3D coordinate generation using templates or coordinate transformations.

4. **Physically refined modeling**  
   Energy minimization, molecular dynamics, force-field validation, and expert chemical inspection.

Benchmark 002 sits between:

```text
Level 2: local topological editing
```

and:

```text
Level 3: approximate geometric perturbation
```

It does not reach:

```text
Level 4: physically refined modeling
```

Therefore, the output should not be treated as a validated HNA ribozyme.

---

## Key Failure Modes

### 1. Short-template scaling failure

A short HNA template cannot automatically provide valid geometry for a large folded RNA.

The 8-mer case was a controlled short-oligomer proof-of-concept. The HH ribozyme is a complex folded RNA architecture.

### 2. Local chemistry vs global fold conflict

Changing sugar/backbone chemistry affects:

- base orientation
- backbone path
- torsion angles
- inter-residue continuity
- steric environment
- tertiary contacts

A local edit can preserve the apparent fold while still producing chemically questionable residues.

### 3. Residue-label ambiguity

Changing a residue label does not necessarily mean the corresponding chemical structure has been correctly built.

For example:

```text
label conversion ≠ chemical conversion
```

This is especially important for nonstandard XNA residues.

### 4. Lack of explicit connectivity

Nonstandard XNA-like residues may require explicit bond definitions.

Without explicit connectivity:

- molecular viewers may infer wrong bonds
- rings may display incorrectly
- topology may be ambiguous
- MD preparation may fail

### 5. No physical relaxation

Large RNA → XNA conversion likely requires:

- energy minimization
- constrained relaxation
- steric clash correction
- torsion optimization
- force-field parameterization
- molecular dynamics validation

---

## Scientific Conclusion

Benchmark 002 is a productive failure.

It shows that Digital Polymerase cannot assume a method that works for an 8-mer will work for a folded ribozyme.

The correct conclusion is:

> Short-mer RNA → HNA template-guided reconstruction is promising, but folded RNA → HNA conversion requires segment-wise, chain-aware, and validation-heavy methods.

## Recommended Future Strategies

### Strategy 1: Segment-wise reconstruction

Split a large RNA into smaller windows:

```text
4-mer
6-mer
8-mer
10-mer
```

Reconstruct each window using compatible XNA templates, then stitch the fragments with continuity constraints.

### Strategy 2: XNA fragment library

Build a library of HNA fragments:

```text
dinucleotide
trinucleotide
tetranucleotide
8-mer
duplex fragments
hairpin-like fragments
```

Use the library to match local geometry rather than forcing a single template.

### Strategy 3: Preserve base frames, rebuild backbone

For functional RNAs, base placement and base-pairing may matter more than preserving every original sugar coordinate.

A future method could:

1. preserve nucleobase planes and key contacts
2. remove or ignore source RNA sugar coordinates
3. rebuild an HNA backbone around the preserved base framework
4. apply constrained minimization

### Strategy 4: Constraint-aware optimization

Large RNA → HNA conversion should eventually enforce constraints such as:

- base-pair preservation
- catalytic-site geometry
- Mg²⁺ or ligand coordination if relevant
- O3′–P chain continuity
- steric clash avoidance
- backbone torsion plausibility

### Strategy 5: MD and force-field integration

No large converted structure should be treated as physically meaningful until it passes appropriate structural refinement and simulation.
